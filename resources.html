The Apostolic Fathers c. AD 70–155

The first generation after the Apostles — the earliest evidence of how the Church worshipped, was governed, and confessed its faith.

Read free: New Advent · Early Christian Writings · Wikisource (ANF I)  ·  Audio: LibriVox

  • Anonymous, c. 90·read ›
    A church manual — baptism, the Eucharist, fasting, and the “two ways” of life and death.
  • Clement of Rome, c. 96·read ›
    Rome writes to Corinth on order and ministry — an early witness to apostolic succession.
  • Ignatius of Antioch, c. 107·read ›
    Written on the road to martyrdom: the bishop, the Eucharist, and the unity of the Church.
  • Polycarp of Smyrna, c. 110·read ›
    A disciple of the Apostle John exhorts the Church to faith and righteousness.
  • Church of Smyrna, c. 155·read ›
    The earliest detailed account of a Christian martyrdom outside the New Testament.
  • Anonymous, c. 130·read ›
    An early reading of the Old Testament as fulfilled in Christ.
  • Mathetes, c. 130·read ›
    A luminous early apology describing Christians as “the soul of the world.”
  • Hermas, c. 140·read ›
    Visions and parables on repentance; treasured and widely read in the early Church.
  • Anonymous, c. 150·read ›
    The earliest surviving Christian sermon.
  • Papias of Hierapolis, c. 110·read ›
    Early traditions about the origins of the Gospels.

The Apologists c. AD 120–200

Defenders who explained the faith to emperors and philosophers, articulating doctrine in dialogue with the wider world.

Read free: New Advent · Early Christian Writings · Wikisource (ANF)  ·  Audio: LibriVox

  • Justin Martyr, c. 155·read ›·listen ›
    A philosopher’s defense — and the earliest description of Sunday worship and the Eucharist.
  • Justin Martyr, c. 160·read ›
    A sustained discussion of Christ in the Hebrew Scriptures.
  • Athenagoras, c. 177·read ›
    Answers the slanders against Christians and defends the resurrection.
  • Theophilus of Antioch, c. 180·read ›
    The first Christian use of the word “Trinity.”
  • Tatian, c. 170·read ›
    A sharp critique of pagan culture; Tatian later compiled the Diatessaron gospel harmony.
  • Minucius Felix, c. 200·read ›
    An elegant Latin dialogue defending the faith against its cultured despisers.

The Ante-Nicene Fathers c. AD 180–300

As the Church spread, these teachers confronted heresy and laid the groundwork of theology, the canon, and the rule of faith.

Read free: New Advent · CCEL (ANF) · Wikisource (ANF)  ·  Audio: LibriVox

  • Irenaeus of Lyons, c. 180·read ›·listen ›
    Refutes Gnosticism and defines apostolic succession and the rule of faith — foundational.
  • Irenaeus of Lyons, c. 190·read ›
    A short, warm catechism of the Christian faith.
  • Tertullian, c. 197·read ›
    “The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church.” The father of Latin theology.
  • Tertullian, c. 200·read ›
    Argues that the Scriptures rightly belong to the Church.
  • Clement of Alexandria, c. 198·read ›
    Faith and learning joined; Christ as the teacher of the whole person.
  • Hippolytus of Rome, c. 215·read ›
    An early church order: ordination, baptism, and the Eucharistic prayer.
  • Origen, c. 225·read ›
    The first attempt at a systematic theology.
  • Origen, c. 248·read ›
    A major reasoned defense of Christianity against a pagan critic.
  • Cyprian of Carthage, c. 251·read ›
    On the episcopate and the grave danger of schism.
  • Gregory Thaumaturgus, c. 260·read ›
    An early Trinitarian creed.

The Nicene Age & the Greek Fathers AD 325–451

The age of the great councils, when the Trinitarian and Christological debates defined the Creed.

Read free: New Advent · CCEL (NPNF) · Wikisource (NPNF)  ·  Audio: LibriVox

  • Athanasius of Alexandria, c. 318·read ›·listen ›
    Why God became man — a perennial introduction to the faith.
  • Athanasius, c. 360·read ›·listen ›
    The book that spread monasticism across the world.
  • Cyril of Jerusalem, c. 350·read ›·listen ›
    Instructions to the newly baptized — an early window into liturgy and creed.
  • Basil the Great, c. 375·read ›·listen ›
    Defends the divinity of the Spirit and the place of tradition in worship.
  • Basil the Great, c. 370·read ›·listen ›
    Homilies on the six days of creation.
  • Gregory of Nazianzus, c. 380·read ›·listen ›
    The most refined defense of the Trinity — earning him the title “the Theologian.”
  • Gregory of Nyssa, c. 385·read ›·listen ›
    A systematic exposition of the faith for teachers.
  • John Chrysostom, c. 388·read ›·listen ›
    On the dignity and the burden of the pastoral office.
  • John Chrysostom, c. 390·read ›
    “Golden-mouthed” preaching on Scripture and the Christian life.

The Latin Fathers AD 340–450

The Western fathers who shaped Latin Christianity, the Scriptures, and the doctrine of grace.

Read free: New Advent · CCEL (NPNF) · Project Gutenberg  ·  Audio: LibriVox

  • Ambrose of Milan, c. 390·read ›·listen ›
    On baptism and the Eucharist, by the bishop who baptized Augustine.
  • Jerome, c. 392·read ›·listen ›
    The translator of the Latin Vulgate and a great scholar of Scripture.
  • Augustine of Hippo, c. 398·read ›·listen ›
    The first spiritual autobiography — “our heart is restless until it rests in Thee.”
  • Augustine, c. 397·read ›·listen ›
    How to read, interpret, and teach the Scriptures.
  • Augustine, c. 426·read ›·listen ›
    History and theology written after the fall of Rome.
  • Augustine, c. 417·read ›·listen ›
    The great Latin synthesis of Trinitarian theology.
  • Vincent of Lérins, c. 434·read ›·listen ›
    “What has been believed everywhere, always, and by all” — a rule for discerning tradition.
  • John Cassian, c. 426·read ›·listen ›
    Brings the wisdom of the Egyptian desert monks to the West.

The Ecumenical Councils & Creeds AD 325–451

Where the Church, gathered as one, confessed the faith in the Creed and the great definitions of the Person of Christ.

Read free: New Advent · CCEL (NPNF II) · Wikisource

  • AD 325·read ›
    Christ “of one essence (homoousios) with the Father,” against Arianism.
  • AD 381·read ›
    Completes the Creed and affirms the divinity of the Holy Spirit.
  • AD 431·read ›
    Mary as Theotokos and the unity of Christ’s person.
  • AD 451·read ›
    Christ acknowledged in two natures, “without confusion or division.”

The Creeds

The Church's confessions of faith, in a few lines.

  • The Nicene–Constantinopolitan Creed (381)
    The Creed of the first two Ecumenical Councils — recited at every Liturgy.
  • The Apostles' Creed
    An ancient baptismal creed of the Western Church.
  • The Athanasian Creed (Quicumque Vult)
    A precise confession of the Trinity and the Incarnation.
  • The Chalcedonian Definition (451)
    Christ in two natures, “without confusion or division.”

The Ecumenical Councils

The canons and definitions of the Church gathered as one.

  • The Seven Ecumenical Councils (canons & decrees)
    Schaff's NPNF Vol. 14 — full canons and dogmatic decrees, with commentary.
  • Nicaea I (325)
    Against Arianism; the original Nicene Creed and twenty canons.
  • Constantinople I (381)
    Completes the Creed; the divinity of the Holy Spirit.
  • Ephesus (431)
    Mary as Theotokos; the unity of Christ.
  • Chalcedon (451)
    The Definition of the two natures of Christ, and thirty canons.

Catechisms

Ordered introductions to the whole of the faith.

  • The Orthodox Faith — Fr. Thomas Hopko free
    A free four-volume introduction from the OCA: doctrine, worship, Bible, spirituality.
  • The Longer Catechism — St. Philaret of Moscow free
    The classic question-and-answer catechism of the Orthodox Church.
  • An Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith — St. John of Damascus free
    The great early synthesis of patristic theology.
  • How to Be an Orthodox Christian (podcast) free
    A free catechetical podcast series for inquirers, from Ancient Faith.

Recommended Reading

A few books to go deeper — free where the text is public-domain.

  • Athanasius — On the Incarnation free
    The classic short treatise on why God became man.
  • The Apostolic Fathers (Lightfoot translation) free
    Clement, Ignatius, Polycarp, the Didache and more, in one free volume.
  • Eusebius — Church History free
    The first history of the Church, from the Apostles to Constantine.
  • Alexander Schmemann — For the Life of the World
    A modern classic on the sacraments and the world as gift.
  • Vladimir Lossky — The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church
    The landmark 20th-century synthesis of Orthodox theology.
  • Kallistos Ware — The Orthodox Way
    A warm, accessible introduction to the Orthodox vision of God.
  • Jaroslav Pelikan — The Emergence of the Catholic Tradition (100–600)
    Volume 1 of the great history of the development of doctrine.
  • Georges Florovsky — The Eastern Fathers of the Fourth Century free
    A free, masterful survey of the Greek Fathers.
